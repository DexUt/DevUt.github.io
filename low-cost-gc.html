Low Cost Gas Chromatograph

I designed and built a bench-top, low-cost gas chromatograph (GC) as a prototype payload concept for a landed Europa mission scenario with ice drilling and a deployable submersible. The system uses air as the carrier gas, a 1 m silicon tube packed with crushed silica gel as the column, a TGS-813 detector, Arduino-based acquisition (100 ms sampling), and real-time visualization in MATLAB. In tests with LP-gas (butane), the GC produced a clear single peak with a short retention time (~5.4 s), demonstrating separation capability while keeping cost and complexity low. The prototype highlights how inexpensive components can illustrate core chromatographic principles and inform future compact instruments for planetary exploration.

Key Details

  • Methods:
    • Used a mini 6 V diaphragm pump to flow ambient air as carrier gas through a packed column.
    • Packed a 1 m silicon tube (ID 5 mm, OD 8 mm) with crushed silica gel to act as the stationary phase.
    • Injected butane via a three-way valve that mixes sample with the carrier before the column; detected eluent with a TGS-813 solid-state sensor.
    • Logged analog output to an Arduino Uno R3 (serial at 9600 baud) and plotted a live chromatogram in MATLAB.
  • Tools:
    • Hardware: Diaphragm pump, silicon tube column + silica gel, three-way valve, TGS-813 gas sensor, Arduino Uno R3, breadboard/wiring, LP-gas sample.
    • Software: Arduino IDE (firmware + serial), MATLAB (real-time parsing/plotting).
  • Outcome:
    Clear detection of butane with a single peak at ~5.4 s retention time in our setup, validating low-cost GC operation. Delivered a replicable, budget-friendly instrument concept aligned with AERO 4842’s Europa payload brief (candidate instrument: GC for elemental/organic analysis). Identified upgrade paths for a flight-like instrument: add a temperature-controlled oven, improve detector sensitivity, automate injection, and regulate flow/pressure for better resolution and repeatability.
